    The Army and Politics in Zimbabwe: Mujuru, the Liberation Fighter and Kingmaker

    by Miles Tendi

    • 16 February 2021
    • Tafadzwa H C Kwaramba

    A book with threads of academic rigour to make the concepts the author puts across more valid and believable. The book has multiple strands, the first is a historical narrative of the internal disputes that rocked the liberation movement. It goes into detail to outline the culture that was present in the military movement. Issues such as the neglect of mental health of liberation fighters after the war is highlighted with the author asserting that Solomon Mujuru was a highly functional alcoholic who never got help for his condition which developed during the war. It is a bit disturbing to think that Zimbabwe's post-independence military was led by a functional alcoholic. Hyper-masculinity is touched upon in the book and the rise of a female, Joice Mujuru, to become vice president of the country is seen through this lens as the author posits that Solomon Mujuru was the leader of the faction that catapulted her to the presidium through creating a quota for women in the ZANU PF presidium. It is therefore worth pondering whether the quotas in their current form are empowering for women. Another concept that the author touched was how Solomon Mujuru asserted himself in spaces with white people by refusing to conform and crack under the "white gaze" by, to an extent, being hyper-masculine and feeding into narratives of black males as hyper-sexual.

    The book steers away from conspiracy theories and uses academic rigour to debunk some myths and assumptions about the liberation movement in Zimbabwe.
