    Becoming Nigerian

    by ElNathan John

    • 08 August 2020
    • Nifemi Akerele

    The 145-page satire chronicles every facet of decadence in the Nigerian system – top to bottom - from politics, religion, governance, tribalism, non-governmental organizations, migration, the artistic burden of being a writer and many more.

    Whilst the author may have adequately discussed a lot of issues, the theme of religion remains constant. Every subject exposed the underlying influence of religion in carrying out a bunch of non religious acts. More so, it beams light on the involvement of religious leaders in perpetrating acts that have sustained corruption in the most populous black nation.

    El Nathan emphasized how most religious leaders conveniently turn a blind eye to elected officials’ failures in delivering on campaign promises. It is commonplace to see religious leaders devotedly pray for politicians to win elections but never maintain the same energy to call them out of their never- ending failures; administration in, administration out.

    The dynamics of religion and spirituality shines throughout the book as the most commonly used Nigerian phrase – God bless your hustle – is used to underscore the influence of a supernatural being, irrespective of religion. God bless your hustle, whether you are in a kind of business that’s legal or not, saves lives, or takes it. It is an anthem, it is acceptable because no one really cares what you do to make a living, especially when you make enough to litter the city with an envoy.

    Be(com)ing Nigerian is indeed a guide to understanding Nigeria and Nigerians, as it identifies individuals, organizations, and political corruption. The book simply says we are guilty of misconduct. Oftentimes, misconduct is associated with politicians, law enforcement agencies, and people in governance. This time, the author shows the complacency of a Nigerian Journalist and the betrayal of the core of their duty. He shows the inhumanity of a mother to a housekeeper, the fraud that non- governmental organizations are, and again, how religion is a safety net for these and more.

    In closing, here is a befitting excerpt from ‘Be(com)ing Nigerian’, ' As a proper Nigerian whose father is God, you must commit all meetings (writings) to His hands. You may work hard but it is God that is in charge of blessing your hustle. Never forget to say at least two prayers in every meeting. One Christian, one Muslim.
