    Oil On Water

    by Helon Habila

    • 16 February 2021
    • Arthur Simbarashe Muzofa

    Oil on Water is a short novel by Nigeria’s 2001 Cain Prize winner Helon Habila. It explores the social, moral and environmental ripple effects of oil extraction along the Niger Delta. Based on the recollections of Rufus, a young journalist hunting his biggest story in very dangerous territory, the novel depicts an African atmosphere heavily tainted by human greed, violence, death and moral degradation.

    In this gripping page-turner; Habila intricately unpacks the complex relationships and dynamics that surround the extraction of oil in Nigeria. Oil is depicted as a serpentine influence that twists and corrodes the integrity of people and the environment they depend on.

    Rufus narrates his investigation into the kidnapping of a white foreign national by a group of militants claiming to be defenders of the environment. Accompanied by his cynical mentor Zaq, Rufus becomes entangled in a bewildering web of lies and violence, all the while assailed by memories of his traumatic past and his bleak existence in an impoverished Nigeria.

    Oil on Water forces the reader to re-evaluate the nature of the much touted notion of economic development. Habila skilfully puts the spotlight on the underlying forces of corruption and deceit that undermine Africa’s best efforts to empower herself through her resources. He reveals the hidden cost of great wealth to be the loss of countless lives in meaningless wars, and the defilement of Africa’s beautiful and rich environment

    A sobering but insightful read for young African’s eager to make an impact on the continent.
