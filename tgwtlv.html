    The Girl with the louding voice

    by Abi Dare

    • 24 September 2020
    • Nifemi Akerele

    Women and girls should be able to determine their own future, no matter where they're born. - Melinda Gates

    14 year old Adunni was forced into a loveless marriage by her father in exchange for money and food. Her dream of getting an education, rising above poverty and making a difference was first cut short after the demise of her mother. Then, her father's failure to step in as the family's breadwinner after the death of his wife further narrowed Adunni's chance of getting an education. Instead, she ends in the home of a local transporter as a third wife to bear him a son and heir.

    Her woes continue after she witnesses the second wife passing and might be lynched by the community because the circumstances surrounding her death are unspeakable. Adunni has no choice other than to leave the village for the city of Lagos where she is once again sold into child labor. Despite the striking difference in settings, her travails deepen in the hands of Big Madam who beats and condemns her to arduous work with little or nothing to eat. She also has to deal with Chief's advances. Her only solace is in Kofi (the Chef), Ms Tia (Madam's neighbor), and her aspirations.

    The novel is not just a story of a girl in distress but girls all over the world, fighting for their right to education; women and girls clamoring for an end to child marriage and child labor. The novel demonstrates the difference education makes and is capable of through the progress made by Adunni.

    While the primary theme of the novel borders on girl-child education, it secondarily discusses child labor, politics, male infertility and child trafficking.

    A louding voice is a coinage by the author for a young woman - Adunni - who confidently shares her perspectives on the world unapologetically about her ambitions an vocal about her feelings, thoughts and ideas. Of the language of the novel, Dare has said: Nigerians speak something called pidgin English, and I knew I didn't want to write in pidgin English because the very educated people speak pidgin English. I wanted it to be non-standard English. I could make it Adunni's. It could be her own English, so to speak.
