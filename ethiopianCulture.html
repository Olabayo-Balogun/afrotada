    Ethiopian culture

    • 01 June 2021
    • Mbugua Karanja

    Background

    Ethiopia is a land-locked country located in Eastern part of Africa bordering Sudan, South Sudan, Kenya, Somalia, Eritrea, Djibouti. The word Ethiopia means the land of burnt-faced people or the land of black people. The country originated from the ancient Aksumite Kingdom from the first century AD. It was ruled predominantly by the Solomonic Dynasty which ruled the Ethiopian Empire from 1270 to 1975 upon the death of Haile Selassie. Ethiopia is a multi-cultural and multi-ethnic country with the second highest population in Africa of about 114 million people. This implies that the country has a diverse set of cultural practises dictated by ethnicity, geographic locations and religion of the Ethiopian people.

    Habesha culture

    Between 1483-1485 according to Diego cao, a Portuguese explorer, the kingdom opened up to Christianity through their ruler and great king NZINGA A NKUWU who also accepted and converted to Christianity with his principal nobles and his citizens opening up a new education system seeing the starting of the first school and an exposure to a new cultural era notable by the changing of the kings native name from NZINGA A NKUWU to JOAO I in honor of the Portuguese king. He would have faced serious challenges due to this especially from his half-brother MPANZU A KITIMA though he overcame him at a battle waged at MBAZA KONGO. It is believed that the king later reverted back to his traditional and cultural beliefs unlike AFONSO I who established Christianity as the state religion of the whole kingdom.

    Religion

    The Country has a deep interest in culture preservation and has conserved their ancient alphabets, calendar, religion, among other practices. It shares some religious practises with the neighbouring nations, Arabs and Mediterranean cultures. The Ethiopian Orthodox Church is one of the oldest religious organizations in the world with its roots dating back to 4 BC. Almost a half of the population belong to the organization, some practice the ancient Judaism beliefs while others are Muslim in religion.

    Ethnic Associations

    Ethiopia has 87 native spoken languages from different ethnic groups spread across the nation. The large ones are the Oromo, the largest ethnic group, who occupy the Oromia region, the Amharas who occupy the Amhara area and the Tigrayans in the Tigray region. The relationship of the tribes has been good for the better part of history despite some political influence that strategically creates division for political gains. Amhara is the official language of the country despite being from an ethnic group that is the second largest.

    Coffee

    Ethiopia is well known for coffee plantations and is the 5th largest coffee-producing country in the world. Therefore coffee forms an essential part of Ethiopian daily activities and most importantly in its culture. Coffee making and drinking is a ritualized process that takes about one hour to create. ‘Buna dabo naw’ is a famous Ethiopian saying that means ‘coffee is our bread’. Coffee establishes a central role in any Ethiopian dietary practices. Coffee in Ethiopia is more than a common beverage drink. ‘Buna Tetu’ is also another famous Amharic phrase meaning ‘Drink Coffee’ which points to the social aspect of Ethiopian coffee just as the western cultures say ‘let’s meet for coffee’. The process has to be systematic from the start to finish. Women or young boys roast coffee from dried raw coffee beans in charcoal. Then the roasted beans are hand-ground and finally added to boiling water. Sugar is added to the mixture in small cups. One is required to inhale the sweet aroma before slowly sipping the coffee.

    Hospitality

    Ethiopians have a welcoming culture where they like entertaining and refreshing their guests with food and drinks. It’s a common practise to remove shoes while entering an Ethiopian house and to dress in a presentable manner. A host sometimes might feed a guest using his hand, a practise known as ‘gursa’ to show oneness. After finishing the meal, the guests wash their hands and are given another round of coffee drinks.
