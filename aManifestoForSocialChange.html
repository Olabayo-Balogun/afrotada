    A Manifesto For Social Change

    by Moeletsi Mbeki

    • 21 September 2020
    • Tafadzwa H C Kwaramba

    The book is a light read that is short and easy to digest and analyses the evolution of the social and economic forces from the time of British colonialism, through Afrikaner nationalism and then to the current epoch of African nationalism in South Africa.

    The author posits that a key difference between the elites who dominated the politics of each epoch lies in land ownership and ownership of capital in general. Mbeki argues that Afrikaner nationalists owned land and dominated agriculture while British colonialists dominated finance and mining, this enabled transitions of political power from the British to the Afrikaners the under the implicit agreement that the Afrikaner nationalists would not interfere with the British colonial interests anchored in migrant labour backed by destruction of peasant agriculture. A similar agreement of non- interference with Afrikaner and British settler interests was reached to enable the 1994 transition to give political power to African nationalists.

    The difficulty, Mbeki argues, is that the African nationalists did not have capital and hence their source of power is the feeding trough of the state and he explains that the emergence of a parasitic black capitalist class co-opted from the resistance movement is because African nationalists gained political power at a point where they had no economic power, this reminded me of Fanon's brilliant essay, "The pitfalls of national consciousness", chapter 3 of the wretched of the earth. This scenario he argues is why the African National Congress (ANC) will find it hard to hand over power if they were to lose an election because, outside of extraction from its incestuous relationship with the state, the elites in the ANC have no other source of economic power. The refusal of liberation movements to vacate power and the use of instruments of coercion to facilitate looting can perhaps be attributed to this phenomenon to an extent.

    Another important aspect of the book is the analysis of the relationship between the broad social classes in South Africa and how the contradictions in these relationships create and unstable basis for a capitalist economy. The key idea being that the current economic set up where a cabal of black business elites mediates between the state and capital and also mediates between capital and labour is dangerous as it creates contradictions that lead to events such as Marikana massacres in instances where the mediation breaks down.

    Overall, a light read and thought provoking material whose lessons can be extrapolated to different countries in Africa.
